Carleton expands on her previous critique of Universal Basic Income (UBI) by exploring how a carefully designed UBI could benefit workers facing AI-driven job displacement, while warning against implementation approaches that reinforce existing power hierarchies.

## Potential Benefits of UBI

The author acknowledges that automation poses genuine threats to employment. Recent college graduates face a 9.3% unemployment rate, and this trend may worsen. A well-structured UBI could provide basic security while allowing individuals to pursue meaningful work or skill development rather than desperate job-seeking.

Carleton references research showing that automation has measurably impacted wages and employment in vulnerable sectors, supporting the case for some form of safety net.

## Critical Design Considerations

The article identifies a major implementation risk: income thresholds that create "cliff effects." If UBI eligibility drops entirely above $50,000 in annual earnings, workers earning $49,999 would refuse raises — trapping themselves in low-wage cycles and damaging long-term career prospects.

She cites evidence from childcare subsidy programs demonstrating exactly this problem, where workers decline promotions to maintain benefits.

## Power Dynamics Concerns

Carleton emphasizes that UBI messaging matters enormously. If framed as a fallback for the unmotivated, it could discourage disadvantaged youth from developing skills. Instead, UBI should empower people to explore talents and pursue growth.

She stresses that implementation must involve diverse voices — economists, engineers, marketers, and affected communities — rather than being designed solely by powerful policymakers.

## Conclusion

While UBI alone cannot solve labor market disruption, thoughtfully designed income support is essential during technological transition. Success requires smooth benefit phase-outs, inclusive policy design, and cultural messaging that encourages ambition rather than dependency.
