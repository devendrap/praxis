Carleton explores whether artificial intelligence can embed economic expertise to help businesses and policymakers make better decisions. She examines how economic knowledge enters AI systems and whether this integration might eventually provide expert-level economic reasoning to non-specialists.

## Current AI Capabilities in Economics

Large language models like ChatGPT can handle undergraduate-level economic optimization problems and explain standard theories. However, they struggle with publication-quality research questions and tend to make unnecessary assumptions when facing complex problems.

## How Economic Knowledge Gets Into AI

The author identifies data labeling companies — including ScaleAI, Surge AI, and Appen — as crucial intermediaries. These firms hire PhD economists and advanced students as contractors, paying "$50-$100 per hour" to evaluate and label economic problems and solutions. This labeled data trains frontier AI models.

## The Scale Challenge

A significant gap exists between available economic knowledge and AI capability. With only one or two PhD economics students per program working as contractors, the profession's vast accumulated knowledge barely scratches the surface of what could be embedded into AI systems.

## Interdisciplinary Necessity

Carleton emphasizes that economics alone isn't sufficient. Machine learning algorithms, psychology, sociology, and political science must combine with economic frameworks to create practical, contextually appropriate solutions for real-world challenges like transit planning or policy decisions.

## The Path Forward

The article concludes that while still theoretical, technological progress makes it increasingly feasible to aggregate expert economic knowledge into AI tools, potentially making sophisticated economic insights accessible to decision-makers beyond specialist economists.
