Carleton, a Princeton economics PhD holder, addresses how high school students can prepare for an uncertain future as AI transforms the job market. She argues that traditional credentials matter less than they once did.

## Two Core Skills for Success

### 1. Articulating Your Value

Students should develop a compelling personal narrative demonstrating problem-solving ability rather than accumulating resume items. Carleton advocates focusing effort on a specific area — like climate tech — through research, hands-on projects, and clear communication about accomplishments. She references Allen Cheng's college admissions advice: "the passionate student who takes a few science AP classes and wins a national science competition is more likely to get into Harvard" than someone with scattered activities.

### 2. Identifying Emerging Opportunities

Students should learn to ask strategic questions about where problems exist and funding flows. She notes that "employment at U.S. VC-backed companies grew 960% from 1990 to 2020," suggesting opportunities concentrate in venture-funded sectors. Networking with professionals to understand industry challenges provides early career advantages.

## Core Argument

Rather than overscheduling themselves, high school students gain more by demonstrating concrete problem-solving skills and tracking where economic opportunities emerge. This approach emphasizes sustainable effort over burnout-inducing resume optimization.
