Carleton examines whether Universal Basic Income (UBI) represents genuine economic security or risks concentrating power among a technological elite. She argues that while UBI emerged as a solution to AI-driven job displacement, the framework could inadvertently create a two-tiered society.

The author references a discussion between entrepreneur Marina Mogliko and LinkedIn CEO Reid Hoffman, where Hoffman suggests that competitive individuals would continue working for differential rewards while others subsist on UBI. Carleton contends this seemingly neutral prediction obscures a troubling reality: "a tiny ruling elite as AI architects" would control the systems governing everyone else.

## Key Concerns

**Power Consolidation**: If most people receive fixed UBI payments while a select few operate AI systems, those system-builders could determine societal rules — from mortgage qualification to credit scores to educational curriculum.

**Literary Precedents**: Carleton contrasts Philip Jose Farmer's 1967 work "Riders of the Purple Wage," which depicted social erosion from mass consumerism, with Marshall Brain's 2003 dystopian novel "Manna." She suggests Brain's techno-dystopia more closely mirrors today's reality, where displaced workers desperately seek employment rather than voluntarily withdrawing from labor.

## The Central Argument

Rather than predicting UBI's likelihood, Carleton urges readers to consider how such systems could concentrate wealth and decision-making power among the already privileged, fundamentally undermining UBI's stated goal of ensuring economic security for everyone.
